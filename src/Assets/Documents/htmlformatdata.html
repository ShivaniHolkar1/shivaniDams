1.10. Accounting for Government Grant and Disclosure of Government Assistance

Description:

Accounting for Government Grants and Disclosure of Government Assistance

Reference:

Ind AS 20 / IAS 20

Purpose:

To ensure proper accounting and disclosure of government grants and disclosure of all other forms of government assistance

1.10.1. Objective and scope:

The objective of this accounting manual is to explain the requirements of Ind AS and highlight specific differences between Ind AS and IFRS, wherever applicable. This manual describes the accounting and disclosure of government grants and disclosure of government assistance.

1.10.2. Definitions:

Particulars

Definition

Government

It refers to government, government agencies and similar bodies whether local, national or international

Government assistance

It pertains to an action by government designed to provide an economic benefit specific to an entity or range of entities qualifying under certain criteria.

Government grants

They are assistance by government in the form of transfer of resources in return for past or future compliance with certain conditions relating to operating activities. But these exclude those:

      • transactions on which reasonable value cannot be placed;
      • transactions with government, which cannot be distinguished from normal trading transactions. For example – Sale of products to Ministry of Defense (Government of India) is a normal trading transaction where Ministry of Defense is a customer.

Grants related to assets

They are government grants wherein primary condition is purchase, construction or otherwise acquisition of long-term assets.

Grants related to income

The government grants other than those related to assets.

1.10.3. Recognition and measurement:

Government grants

Government grants, including non-monetary grants at fair value, shall not be recognised until there is reasonable assurance that:

    • the conditions attached to the grant will be complied with; and
    • the grants will be received.

Receipt of a grant does not by itself, provide conclusive evidence that the conditions pertaining to the grant have been or will be fulfilled.

The manner in which a grant is received does not affect the accounting method to be adopted in regard to the grant.

The benefit of a government loan at a below-market rate of interest is treated as a government grant.

This type of grant shall be accounted for in accordance with requirement of Ind AS 109 (Refer Accounting Manual “Ind AS 109: Financial Instruments”).

Once a government grant is recognised, any related contingent liability or contingent asset is treated in accordance with Ind AS 37 (Refer Accounting Manual “Ind AS 37: Provisions, Contingent Liabilities and Contingent Assets”).

Government grants shall be recognised in profit or loss on a systematic basis over the periods in which the expenses are recognised as the related costs for which the grants are intended to compensate.

Grants related to income are presented as a part of profit or loss, either separately or under a general heading such as 'Other income' or alternatively, they are deducted in reporting the related expense

Other points for consideration:

  • Grants pertaining to recognition of specific expense are recognised Statement of profit and loss in the same relevant period.
  • Grants related to depreciable assets are usually recognised in profit or loss over the periods and in the proportion in which depreciation expense on those assets is recognised.
  • A government grant that becomes receivable as compensation for expenses or losses already incurred or for the purpose of giving immediate financial support to the entity with no future related costs shall be recognised in profit or loss of the period in which it becomes receivable.
  • Grants related to non-depreciable assets (example, freehold land) may also require the fulfilment of certain obligations and would then be recognised in Statement of profit and loss over the periods that bear the cost of meeting the obligations.

Non-monetary government grants

Following two alternatives are available:

      • Fair valuation of non-monetary asset and account for grant and asset at that fair value (E.g. land and other resources); or
      • Record both asset and grant at nominal value.

Presentation of grants related to assets

Grant related to asset, including non-monetary asset at fair value, shall be presented in balance sheet as either of the following –

      • Setting up grant as deferred income and the same is recognised in profit or loss on a systematic basis over the useful life of the asset.
      • Deducting the grant in arriving at the carrying amount of asset - grant recognised in profit or loss over depreciable life of the asset as a reduced depreciation expense.

Purchase of asset and receipt of related grants are generally shown separately in the Statement of cash flows.

ABC receives EPCG benefit on the import of certain capital assets under the condition that it will meet the export obligation under the EPCG scheme. The benefit received is accounted as a government grant and hence required to be included in the cost of the asset with the corresponding recognition of grant income as and when the export obligation is met. For e.g. when purchasing an asset worth Rs. 100, ABC is liable to pay Rs. 20 as custom duty (total cost of the asset being Rs. 120). Under the EPCG scheme, Rs. 20 duty is waived off. Under Ind AS, the asset is recognized at Rs. 120 and a corresponding liability of Rs. 20 is recognised.

Presentation of grants related to income

Grant related to income are presented as part of Statement of profit and loss in either of the following manner-

      • Separately in Statement of profit and loss or under general heading 'other income'; or
      • Deducted from the related expense.

Disclosure of the grant is necessary for a proper understanding of the financial statements.

Repayment of government grants

Grant that becomes repayable shall be accounted for as a change in accounting estimate. (Refer Accounting Manual “Ind AS 8: Accounting Policies, Changes in Accounting Estimates and Errors”).

Circumstances giving rise to repayment of a grant related to an asset may require consideration to be given to the possible impairment of the new carrying amount of the asset.

Applied first against any unamortised deferred credit for that grant

Increasing the carrying amount of asset or reducing the deferred income balance by amount payable

Excess over deferred credit or if no deferred credit exists, to be recognised in profit or loss

The situation of repayment of grant is unlikely. However, if such situation arises, the accounting shall be done as mentioned above.

Government assistance

These are types of assistance on which reasonable value cannot be placed such as free technical or marketing advice; or transactions with Government, which cannot be distinguished from normally activity (Example: Government procurement policy that is responsible for portion of total sales).

1.10.4. Disclosure:

The following matters shall be disclosed:

    • the accounting policy adopted for government grants, including the method of presentation adopted in the financial statements;
    • the nature and extent of government grants recognised in the financial statements and an indication of other forms of government assistance which led to a direct benefit; and
    • Unfulfilled conditions and other contingencies attached to government assistance that has been recognised.

1.10.5. GAAP differences between Ind AS and IFRS:

  • IAS 20 gives an option to measure non-monetary government grants either at their fair value or at nominal value. Ind AS 20 requires measurement of such grants only at their fair value. Thus, the option to measure these grants at nominal value is not available under Ind AS 20.
  • IAS 20 gives an option to present the grants related to assets, including nonmonetary grants at fair value in the balance sheet either by setting up the grant as deferred income or by deducting the grant in arriving at the carrying amount of the asset. Ind AS 20 requires presentation of such grants in balance sheet only by setting up the grant as deferred income.

The above GAAP differences no longer exist (as per amendment dated September 20, 2018). This accounting manual topic Ind AS 20 is prepared after considering this amendment and the GAAP differences have been presented here only for reference.
